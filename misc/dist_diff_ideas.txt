Ideas (categorized by "theme", ideas without datasets marked):

## Deception
Explain differences in transcript when someone is lying. Example hypotheses:
- "uses short words"
- "uses lots of qualifiers" ("like", "probably", "maybe")

Example datasets/applications:
- The Columbia SRI Colorado Deception Corpus: https://catalog.ldc.upenn.edu/LDC2013S09
- Labeled transcripts from the game show, diplomacy: https://go.umd.edu/diplomacy_data
- Tuin and Rooijackers (2021) - Labeled transcripts from Among Us gameplay: https://ieee-cog.org/2021/assets/papers/paper_249.pdf
- Bachenko et al. (2008) - Civil and criminal interview transcripts: https://aclanthology.org/C08-1006.pdf
- Newman et al. (2003) - https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/0146167203029005010

## Gender/racial biases
Explain differences in descriptions of different racial groups or genders. Example hypotheses:
- Uses more negative terms.
- Is more paternalistic.
- Describes someone as dangerous.

Example datasets/applications:
- Merullo et al. (2019) - racism in football commentary: https://arxiv.org/pdf/1909.03343.pdf
- Rice et al. (2019) - racism in judicial opinions: https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1177/2053168019848930
- Cai et al. (2021) - gender bias in judicial rulings: https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=3869294
- Gendered language in ratemyprofessor reviews: http://benschmidt.org/profGender

## Cross-time analysis
Identifies changes in discussions over time. Example hypotheses:
- "talks about COVID"
- "talks about Trump"

Example datasets/applications:
- Dzogang et al. (2017) - differences in news coverage over time: https://datadryad.org/resource/doi:10.5061/dryad.nh775

## Political
Features of the political process. Example hypotheses:
- "uses politically neutral language"
- "uses more negative terms"

Example datasets/applications:
- Yano et al. (2015) - which bills are likely to survive: www.cs.cmu.edu/~nasmith/papers/yano+smith+wilkerson.naacl12.pdf
- Differences in how left/right articles speak: https://www.crowdflower.com/data-for-everyone/
- Demzsky et al. (2019) - Differences in Twitter commentary: https://nlp.stanford.edu/pubs/demszky2019analyzing.pdf

## Business
Explaining financial movements via consumer sentiment. Example hypotheses:
- "less confident"
- "talks about COVID"
- "talks about Ukraine"

Example datasets/applications:
- Brown (2012) - Twitter sentiment during bull vs. bear markets: https://www.researchgate.net/publication/271702125_Bulls_Bearsand_Birds_Studying_the_Correlation_between_Twitter_Sentiment_and_the_SP500
- Jiazheng et al. (2020) - earnings conference call dataset: https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3340531.3412879

## Interpersonal
Explain differences in individual conversations. Example hypotheses:
- "is hostile"
- "is sarcastic"

Example datasets/applications:
- Price et al. (2020) - annotated conversation styles: https://aclanthology.org/2020.alw-1.15.pdf
- Chakravarthula et al. (2018) - couples in therapy, with annotations on outcomes: https://arxiv.org/abs/1805.09436
- Hate speech classification: https://github.com/t-davidson/hate-speech-and-offensive-language
- Jia (2020) - Annotated call center transcripts: https://aclanthology.org/2020.ecnlp-1.4.pdf

## Consumer segmentation
Identifies differences in text features between consumer segments. Example hypotheses:
- "enjoys the premium features"
- "likes the flexibility"
- "wants more options"
- "wants cheaper pricing"

Example datasets/applications:
- Pitt et al. (2020) - exploring differences between consumer segments via text: https://www.diva-portal.org/smash/get/diva2:1399516/FULLTEXT01.pdf

## Learning/feedback
What's the difference between a good and bad essay? A good or bad interview? Example hypotheses:
- "uses longer words"
- "talks more about concepts"

Example datasets/applications:
- The Hewlett Foundation: Automated Essay Scoring: https://www.kaggle.com/c/asap-aes
- Mathias and Bhattacharyya (2018) - enriched essay grade dataset: https://www.cse.iitb.ac.in/~sam/papers/SAM-LREC-2018-ASAP++.pdf
- MIT Interview Datset: https://roc-hci.com/past-projects/automated-prediction-of-job-interview-performances/

## Search tools
How does one subject/article differ from another? Example hypotheses:
- "talks more about finance than economics"
- "is more technical"
- "discusses transfer learning"

Example datasets/applications:
- Kwary (2018) - academic journal articles: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2352340917306194
- Murakami (2017) - journal articles labeled by topic: https://www.euppublishing.com/doi/full/10.3366/cor.2017.0118?src=recsys


## Popularity
What are popular movies/bloggers/writers doing differently?
- "has more characters"
- "stays on topic"
- "has more references"

Example datasets/applications:
- Cornell Movie Dialogue Corpus: http://www.cs.cornell.edu/~cristian/Cornell_Movie-Dialogs_Corpus.html
- Blog authorship corpus: https://www.kaggle.com/rtatman/blog-authorship-corpus/activity
- Good vs bad wikipedia writing: https://www.tensorflow.org/datasets/catalog/wikipedia

## Model Performance
Assist researchers with explaining why models perform worse on some data.
- "asks about personal details"
- "looking for beauty products"
- "asks for personal advice"
- "asks multiple questions"

Example datasets/applications:
- Which types of questions does the Watson/Jeopardy bot (or other Q/A bots) get wrong?
- Why are some search results worse?: https://www.kaggle.com/c/home-depot-product-search-relevance/data
- Chatbot failures - on which queries does Siri/Alexa/etc. perform the worst?: http://alumni.soe.ucsc.edu/~waiting/umpublished/Exploring%20the%20effect%20of%20dataset%20on%20chatbot%20performance.pdf
